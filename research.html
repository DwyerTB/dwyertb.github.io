Research

/rɪˈsɜrtʃ/

I am deeply interested in how children learn their native language, and how language is represented and processed in the mind and brain. In particular, I am interested in computationally modeling language acquisition, especially morphology and semantics, using algorithmic approaches. Further, I believe that if we are able to algorithmically model the process of language acquisition in the mind and brain, then we may employ these models to create more robust language technologies. Much of current Natural Language Processing uses machine learning techniques over large and highly-saturated datasets, which poses a stark contrast to the relatively small, sparse data to which children are exposed. Incorporating models of language acquisition, then, will allow us to vastly reduce the amount of data upon which we train. This will be especially useful for low-resource languages and for mitigating bias in large-scale models. As such, my research interests lie at the intersection of computational linguistics, language acquisition, psycholinguistics, and cognitive modelling. I see the relationship between these areas of study as mutually beneficial: we may employ cognitive methodology to inform the design of NLP tools, but we may also employ computational tools to learn more about the human language faculty.

Research


Natural Language Processing
I came to Penn interested mainly in Natural Language Processing, and began working with Dr. Chris Callison-Burch my freshman year. I collaborated with Minh Nguyen to develop multimodal word embeddings in spring 2019. In summer 2019, I was funded by the Penn Undergraduate Research Mentoring Program (PURM) to continue this project. However, we quickly realized that we struggled to come up with embeddings for anything that was not a single word, and so my project developed into one on semantic compositionality . I worked with several amazing people in the summer of 2019, including Sherry Shi, who was also funded by PURM. You can find an article on this project here . In fall 2019, I continued work on a related area, namely the ability to map adjectives to visual transformations on images, similarly to how adjectives can be represented as matrices for written words. This work is ongoing.

Computational Phonology
As I became increasingly interested in the computational and cognitive underpinnings of language acquisition, I began working with Dr. Daniel Swingley on the development of phonological embeddings that can be used in models of language acquisition. We aim to use bottleneck features to extract embeddings based on a wide variety of linguistic input that can be used to model a newborn baby's phonological perception. This work is a collaboration with Dr. Emmanuel Dupoux and Dr. Thomas Schatz . In January 2020, I was funded by MindCORE to travel to the University of Maryland to work with Dr. Schatz and Dr. Naomi Feldman to further develop this project. This project is still in progress, with the goal of in-person collaboration with Dr. Dupoux in the near future.

Computational Morphology and Cognitive Approaches
I originally became interested in the relationship between human language processing and computational language tools while studying with Dr. Charles Yang. While taking his class, I conducted a study with my roommate's Seeing Eye Dog (thanks Kay and Zenith!) about evidence against fast-mapping in dogs. I later presented the results of this study and the Berkeley SLUgS Linguistics Symposium . In spring 2020, I began working with Dr. Yang and Dr. Jordan Kodner on computational morphology. Namely, we wish to derive a cognitively-motivated computational model of morphological acquisition. In summer 2020, I will be working at the Information Sciences Institute on the ADAM project with Dr. Yang, Dr. Ryan Gabbard, and others.

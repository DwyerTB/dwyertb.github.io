Research

/rɪˈsɜrtʃ/

I am deeply interested in how children learn their native language, and how language is represented and processed in the mind and brain. In particular, I am interested in computational modelling of language acquisition, especially morphology and semantics, using algorithmic approaches. Further, I believe that if we are able to algorithmically model the processes of language acquisition in the mind and brain, then we may employ these models to create more robust language technologies. Much of current Natural Language Processing uses machine learning techniques over large and highly-saturated datasets, which poses a stark contrast to the relatively small, sparse data to which children are exposed. Incorporating models of language acquisition, then, will allow us to vastly reduce the amount of data upon which we train. This will be especially useful for low-resource languages and for mitigating bias in large-scale models. As such, my research interests lie at the intersection of computational linguistics, psycholinguistics, and cognitive modelling. I see the relationship between these areas of study as mutually beneficial: we may employ cognitive methodology to inform the design of NLP tools, but we may also employ computational tools to learn more about the human language faculty.

A complete list of my publications may be viewed via Semantic Scholar or Google Scholar .


Current Work

Computational Morphology
I originally became interested in the relationship between human language processing and computational language tools while studying with Dr. Charles Yang. In spring 2020, I began working with Dr. Yang and Dr. Jordan Kodner on computational morphological segmentation and generation. Namely, we wish to derive a cognitively-motivated computational model of morphological acquisition that can be applied to NLP tasks and demonstrate that this model can succeed on sparse input like that which a child might experience.


Past Work

DARPA GAILA Project
During the summer of 2020, I was a research assistant at the Information Sciences Institute working on the ADAM project, ISI's effort for DARPA's Grounded Artificial Intelligence Language Acquisition (GAILA) program. Our goal is to demonstrate success in language acquisition from cognitively-plausible perception-language pairings; my work specifically focuses on implementation of the program in Chinese and the ablation of various perceptual cues. This program is a collaboration with Dr. Ryan Gabbard, Dr. Marjorie Freedman, Dr. Mitch Marcus, Dr. Charles Yang, and others. The ADAM code is open-sourced with the goal of providing a "sandbox" for future researchers in this area.

Computational Phonology
As I became increasingly interested in the computational and cognitive underpinnings of language acquisition, I began working with Dr. Daniel Swingley on the development of phonological embeddings that can be used for the modelling of concepts in phonological learning such as categorical perception. We aim to use bottleneck features to extract embeddings based on a wide variety of linguistic input that can be used to model a newborn baby's phonological perception. This work is a collaboration with Dr. Emmanuel Dupoux and Dr. Thomas Schatz . In January 2020, I was funded by MindCORE to travel to the University of Maryland to work with Dr. Schatz and Dr. Naomi Feldman to further develop this project. This project is still in progress, with the goal of in-person collaboration with Dr. Dupoux in the near future.

Multi-modal Approaches and Compositionality
I came to Penn interested mainly in Natural Language Processing, and began working with Dr. Chris Callison-Burch my freshman year. I collaborated with Minh Nguyen to develop multimodal word embeddings in spring 2019. In summer 2019, I was funded by the Penn Undergraduate Research Mentoring Program (PURM) to continue this project. However, we quickly realized that we struggled to come up with embeddings for anything that was not a single word, and so my project developed into one on semantic compositionality . I worked with several amazing people in the summer of 2019, including Sherry Shi, who was also funded by PURM; you can find an article on this project here . In fall 2019, I continued work on a related area, namely the ability to map adjectives to visual transformations on images, similarly to how adjectives can be represented as matrices for written words. The code for this project can be found here.
